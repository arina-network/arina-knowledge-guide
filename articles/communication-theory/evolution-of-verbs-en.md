# Evolution of Verbs: from Signals to Hierarchical Processes
_Author [Slava Zemlianskyi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/zemlianskyi/), published 2026-04-18 in [Arina Knowledge Guide](https://github.com/arina-network/arina-knowledge-guide)_

Verbs are usually treated as a grammatical category — a way to describe actions, states, and events. But this view misses something deeper: verbs are not just linguistic elements, they are structures for representing change over time.

This article does not attempt to reconstruct the exact historical evolution of verbs in human languages. Real linguistic history is messy, non-linear, and shaped by many parallel developments. Instead, what follows is a conceptual model — a way to understand how verbs can evolve as tools for encoding increasingly complex processes.

We can think of this evolution as a progression:
- from immediate action signals
- to descriptions of observable processes
- to memory of past events
- to sequences and causal chains
- to predictions and plans
- and finally to structured, hierarchical processes

At each step, verbs gain expressive power. They move from triggering behavior to describing reality, then to modeling it, and eventually to organizing it.

In this sense, verbs are not just about language. They are an interface between cognition and time — and ultimately, a way to represent processes that can be shared, reused, and composed.

## Primitive Calls to Action: flat reality

At the beginning, verbs were indistinguishable from signals:
- “Run!”
- “Come!”
- “Stop!”
These are not descriptions — they are direct triggers for behavior.

Each verb corresponds to a single, atomic action. There is no structure:
- no time abstraction, only now
- no representation, only execution
- no decomposition/composition 
- no hierarchy

Language here functions like a real-time control system. Every action is isolated. The world is “flat” — just immediate impulses transmitted between love creatures.

## Observable Processes: repeated units
As perception became more structured, verbs began to describe ongoing processes rather than trigger actions:
- "Wind blows”
- “Water flows”
- “Fire burns”

Verbs now represent repeatable processes, but still mostly as indivisible wholes. This introduces:
- represent repeatable patterns
- detach from immediate urgency
- enable shared observation

This is the birth of the present indicative — a way to encode stable truths about the environment. Language shifts from:
- Command -> Description
- Reaction -> Representation

But still:
- No internal structure of the process is expressed

“Burning” is observed, but not decomposed.

## Observable Processes in the Past: encoding experience

The introduction of past tense marks a major cognitive leap:
- “It rained”
- “The animal ran”
- “We found food”

Now verbs capture:
- completed actions
- experiences no longer observable
- cause-effect relationships

This enables:
- knowledge transfer
- learning from experience
- collective memory

The verb becomes a container for what has happened, not just what is happening.

## Sequences of Actions in the Past: narratives and causality with linear composition

A further step is not just describing isolated past events, but ordering them:
- “We went to the river, caught fish, and returned home”
- “He fell, got up, and continued running”

Now verbs can be chained:
> Process A -> Process B -> Process C

This creates:
- temporal sequencing
- implicit causality
- narrative structure

This is the foundation of storytelling — but also of reasoning:
- What happened first?
- What led to what?

However, structure is still flat and sequential:
- no nesting
- no abstraction
- just ordered lists

Language begins to simulate time as a sequence, not just a set of points.

## Expected Processes in the Future: prediction

With future-oriented verbs, language begins to model events that do not yet exist:
- “It will rain”
- “The sun will rise”
- “They will come”

This introduces:
- prediction based on patterns
- expectation of outcomes
- shared anticipation

Verbs now encode probabilistic models of reality. This allows live creatures to:
- prepare
- anticipate risks
- align expectations

## Planned Actions in the Future: intentional coordination and grouping actions

Beyond prediction comes intention:
- “We will build a shelter”
- “I will go hunting”
- “We will meet at dawn”

Here verbs express:
- goals
- plans
- commitments

This is a crucial distinction:
- Prediction: what is likely to happen
- Plan: what we intend to make happen

The humans started grouping actions:
> “We will hunt and then build shelter”

Here we can see implicit grouping:
- multiple actions treated as a unit
- early notion of a “plan”

This is the first step toward hierarchy:
- a plan is a higher-level process
- it contains lower-level actions

Language becomes a tool for:
- coordinating complex group actions
- synchronizing behavior across time

The verb evolves into a contract between humans. But the structure is still mostly informal.

## Actions as Shared Knowledge: from Language to Process Systems

At the most advanced stage, verbs no longer just describe or plan actions — they define structured, reusable processes. This is where natural language begins to resemble ormal systems.

### Conditional Processes (if–else)

Examples:
- “If it rains, we stay inside”
- “If the animal runs, chase it; otherwise, wait”

Verbs now encode:
- branching logic
- alternative futures
- decision rules

This allows:
- adaptability
- strategy under uncertainty

Language becomes capable of expressing multiple possible realities.

### Repeatable Processes (while / until / for each)

Examples:
- “While there is light, we work”
- “Until the fire goes out, keep watch”
- “For each tree, collect fruit”

Here verbs define:
- iteration
- persistence over time
- operations over sets

This enables:
- routine formation
- efficient resource use
- scalable behavior

Language now captures loops in time.

### Building Processes (create / modify / destroy)

Examples:
- “Build a shelter”
- “Fix the tool”
- “Destroy the barrier”

Verbs here encode:
- transformation of the environment
- state changes in objects
- directed manipulation of reality

This reflects:
- engineering thinking
- tool use and construction
- intentional world modification

Language becomes a system for describing how to change the state of the world.

### Logic and Repetition

With conditionals and loops it creates non-linear structure, but still mostly at one level.
- control flow
- reusable patterns

But the key missing piece is explicit nesting of processes inside processes.

## Hierarchy of Processes: actions eithin actions

This is the crucial step. Language begins to express that:
> A process can consist of other processes, which themselves consist of smaller actions.

Examples:
- “Build a shelter”
    - “Lay the foundation”
    - “Construct the walls”
    - “Install the roof”
- “Prepare for winter”
    - “Gather food”
    - “Build a shelter”
    - “Store resources”

Here:
> “Build” is not atomic. It is a composite verb.

This introduces decomposition, composition and nesting.

### Decomposition (top-> down)

High-level verbs break into sub-processes:
- “Cook dinner” -> cut, boil, mix, serve
- “Hunt” -> track, chase, capture

This enables:
- teaching complex tasks
- delegation
- understanding systems

### Composition (bottom -> up)

Lower-level actions combine into higher-level ones:
- “Cut + heat + mix” -> “cook”
- “Plan + gather + assemble” -> “build”

This creates:
- abstraction layers
- reusable “modules” of behavior

### Nesting

Processes can contain other processes recursively:

- “Organize a trip”
    - “Plan route”
        - “Check map”
        - “Estimate time”
    - “Prepare supplies”

This is no longer just language — it is a tree structure of actions.

## Verbs as Interfaces to process layers

At this stage, verbs act like interfaces to different levels of abstraction:
- low-level verbs:
    - “move”, “cut”, “lift”
- mid-level verbs:
    - “build”, “cook”, “write”
high-level verbs:
    - “organize”, “manage”, “create”

Each level:
- hides internal complexity
- exposes only what is necessary

This is similar to:
- Organizational structures
- Engineering systems
- Software architecture

## Hierarchy + Control Flow = Full Process Systems

When hierarchy combines with earlier constructs:
- Conditionals operate on processes:
    - “If food is low → start hunting process”
- Loops operate on processes:
    - “Repeat gathering until storage is full”
- Plans contain nested processes:
    - “Prepare for winter” → multiple coordinated sub-systems

Now language can describe:
- complex adaptive systems
- multi-step strategies
- coordinated group behavior

## From Verbs to Programs

At this point, natural language reaches something very powerful. It can express:
- hierarchy (nested processes)
- control flow (if, while)
- state change (create, modify, destroy)

which are exactly the core components of:
- organized systems
- programs
- algorithms

Example:
> “If winter is coming, gather food until storage is full, then build shelter and reinforce it.”

This is:
- conditional
- iterative
- hierarchical

A full process specification.

## Conclusion: verbs as hierarchical model of processes

Verbs evolved from signals into model of processes. At their most advanced, they allow us to:
- describe reality
- remember it
- predict it
- plan it
- and systematically **transform it**

The evolution of verbs can be seen as a progression:
- from atomic actions
- to sequences
- to structured flows
- to hierarchical systems of processes

The key final step is hierarchy:
> The ability to treat an action as something that can be broken down — or built up.

With this, language becomes capable of representing:
- not just what happens
- but how complex systems of processes are organized

In this sense, verbs no longer just about description of processes through the passage of time. They carry a model of change — sometimes simple, sometimes deeply structured — that allows us not only to describe reality, but to organize and reshape it.

And through them, natural language becomes a tool not just for communication, but for designing reality itself.
