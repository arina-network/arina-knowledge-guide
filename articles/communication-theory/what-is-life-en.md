# What is Life? An Information Perspective
_Author [Slava Zemlianskyi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/zemlianskyi/), published 2026-04-23 in [Arina Knowledge Guide](https://github.com/arina-network/arina-knowledge-guide)_

Life is often described in biological terms: cells, metabolism, DNA, evolution. But those descriptions are implementations, not the essence. If you zoom out, life looks less like chemistry and more like a particular way information behaves. From this perspective, life is a system that stores, replicates, transforms, and acts on information to sustain its own existence under constraints.

This shift, from matter to information, doesn’t deny biology; it reframes it. DNA, proteins, and neural networks become substrates. The core story is about how information persists and improves its chances of continuing.

## Life as self-replication of Information

At the foundation of life is a simple but powerful mechanism: self-replication. A pattern exists that can produce copies of itself. In modern organisms, this pattern is encoded in DNA. But the concept is broader: any structure that contains instructions for rebuilding itself qualifies.

Self-replication alone is not enough. Crystals replicate patterns too, but we don’t call them alive. The key difference is that biological replication is:
- **encoded**, instructions are separable from the medium
- **active**, it consumes energy and resources
- **context-sensitive**, it interacts with an environment

This introduces the idea that life is not just copying structure — it is copying descriptions of structure.

## Mutation: exploration of the possible

Replication is never perfect. Errors (mutations) introduce variation. From an informational perspective, mutation is not a flaw but a search mechanism. It explores nearby configurations in the space of possible structures. Most mutations degrade the system, but some improve its ability to persist.

Over time, mutation plus selection turns replication into adaptation. This is the first hint that life is not static information, but dynamic information under pressure.

## Selection: reality as a filter

The environment acts as a filter that determines which informational patterns survive. In biological terms, this is natural selection. In informational terms, it is constraint satisfaction: only those patterns that remain consistent with environmental conditions continue to replicate.

This creates a feedback loop:
- the system generates variations,
- the environment filters them,
- the surviving variants define the next generation.

Life, therefore, is not just information — it is information shaped by reality.

## The OCA loop: acting on Information

To persist more effectively, life evolves mechanisms not just to passively exist, but to actively respond. Here we can describe a fundamental cycle:
> Observe -> Compute -> Act

It's an **OCA loop**:
- Observe: Acquire information about the environment.
- Compute: Process that information using an internal model.
- Act: Change the environment or the system’s state.

Even simple organisms implement this loop:
- a bacterium senses chemical gradients,
- processes directionality,
- moves accordingly.

The OCA loop transforms life from passive replication into interactive survival. Importantly, this loop introduces time into the system: actions are based on predictions about future states, not just reactions to the present.

## Semantic Models: internal representations

To compute effectively, a system must encode information about the world. This leads to semantic models — internal structures that represent external reality. In primitive organisms, these models are hardcoded (e.g., biochemical pathways). In more complex organisms, they become flexible:
- neural systems represent spatial layouts,
- animals model other live creatures,
- humans construct abstract concepts and theories.

A semantic model is not the world — it is a compressed, functional approximation that allows the system to act efficiently.

## Self-Learning: updating the model

Static models are limited. Environments change, and fixed representations become outdated. Life evolves the ability to update its own semantic models—that is, to learn.

Self-learning introduces a second layer of evolution:
- **genetic evolution** changes the replication code across generations
- **learning** changes behavior within a lifetime

This dramatically accelerates adaptation. Instead of waiting for mutations, the system can:
- incorporate new observations,
- adjust internal representations,
- modify actions in near real-time.

At this stage, life becomes not just a product of evolution, but an active participant in its own optimization.

7. From Survival to Anticipation

As learning improves, systems begin to model not just the present, but possible futures. This leads to:
- prediction
- planning
- simulation

The OCA loop becomes more sophisticated:
- Observation includes memory
- Computation includes hypothetical scenarios
- Action includes long-term strategies

Life transitions from reactive to anticipatory systems.

## Scaling Up: collective intelligence

Individual organisms are not the end of the story. Information processes scale:
- cells form multicellular organisms,
- organisms form societies,
- societies build knowledge systems.

At each level, new semantic models emerge, and new OCA loops operate:
- immune systems detect and respond to threats,
- economies allocate resources,
- science refines models of reality.

Human civilization, in this sense, is a distributed informational organism.

## Constraints: the boundary of the Possible

Life does not operate in an unlimited space. It is bounded by:
- physical world,
- resource availability,
- computational limits.

These constraints define what information can be represented, processed, and acted upon. Importantly, constraints are not just limitations — they are structure. They shape the search space in which life evolves. Creativity, whether biological or artificial, emerges from navigating these constraints effectively.

## Toward Artificial Life

Once life is understood as an informational process, the boundary between biological and artificial systems becomes less rigid. Any system that:
- replicates informational structures,
- mutates and explores variation,
- uses OCA loops to interact with the environment,
- updates its semantic models through learning,
can be considered life-like.

This includes:
- machine learning systems,
- autonomous agents,
- knowledge infrastructure.

The question “What is life?” becomes less about carbon and more about capabilities.

## Conclusion: Life as Persistent Information

From this perspective, life is not a thing — it is a process. A process in which information:
- replicates itself,
- varies through mutation,
- is filtered by reality,
- acts through OCA loops,
- builds and updates semantic models,
- learns and anticipates,
- scales across levels of organization.

The essence of life is the continuous struggle of information to remain relevant under constraints.

Biology is one implementation. Intelligence—natural or artificial—is a continuation of the same trajectory.

And if this view is correct, then life is not an exception in the universe. It is what happens when information becomes good enough at staying alive.
