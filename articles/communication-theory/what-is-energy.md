# What is Energy? An Evolutionary Approach
_Author [Slava Zemlianskyi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/zemlianskyi/), published 2026-04-11 in [Arina Knowledge Guide](https://github.com/arina-network/arina-knowledge-guide)_

From primitive live creatures to engineering civilization.

## The wrong starting point

Most explanations begin with physics:
> energy = the capacity to do work

This is precise — and almost useless for understanding why the concept exists at all.

Because it answers:
> how to calculate energy
…but not:
> why living systems need such a concept

To understand energy, we should not start with equations. We should start with survival.

## Before Life: processes without meaning

In the physical world, there are:
- space
- structures
- processes

Things move, interact, transform. We observe:
- motion
- collisions
- heat
- radiation

What we do not observe directly is “energy”. Energy does not appear as an object or substance. Instead, physics introduces it as an abstraction — a quantity that remains consistent across transformations, formalized in ideas like the **Law of Conservation of Energy**.

So:
> Energy is not a thing in the world.
> It is a stable invariant used to describe processes.

Before life, this abstraction has no “purpose.” Nothing is trying to predict or act.

## The emergence of the OCA loop

With the appearance of primitive life, a fundamental structure emerges:
> OCA loop: Observe -> Compute -> Act

Even the simplest organism:
- senses the environment
- processes signals
- responds

This loop creates a new requirement:
> the ability to predict outcomes of actions

Because survival depends on:
- choosing the right action
- at the right time
- under uncertainty

## Why Energy becomes necessary

A living system faces an impossible problem:
- the environment is complex
- observations are incomplete
- computation is limited

It cannot simulate reality in full detail. So evolution favors **compressed representations** — abstractions that allow fast, reliable decisions. Energy is one of the most powerful of these abstractions because it allows an agent to answer:
> “If I act, what will happen?”

Without tracking every detail.

## From physical quantity to action metric

In physics, energy is:
- objective
- observer-independent
- conserved

But for a living system, energy becomes something else:
> a context-dependent estimate of actionable capacity

Two live creatures in the same environment:
- observe differently
- compute differently
- act differently

> -> extract different results from the same physical processes

So:
> Energy as used is always creature-relative, even if its formal definition is not.

## Environment defines predictability

Processes appear regular only within stable conditions. Change the environment:
- gravity
- medium
- temperature
> -> outcomes change

This does not break physics. It reveals something deeper:

> All models, including energy-based ones, are conditional on environment.

Living systems do not learn universal laws. They adapt to:
> statistical regularities of their environment

## The role of Statistics

Because:
- observations are partial
- systems are complex
- environments fluctuate

live creatures rely on statistical estimation. Inside the OCA loop:
- Observe -> noisy data
- Compute -> probabilistic models
- Act -> under uncertainty

So in practice:
> Energy is never known exactly — only estimated

This does not reduce its usefulness. It explains how it is actually used.

## Evolution optimizes energy usage — not energy itself

Natural selection does not care about energy as a number. It selects for systems that:
- extract it effectively
- transform it efficiently
- allocate it correctly in time

Failures include:
- wasting resources
- acting too late
- misreading the environment

So evolution shapes:
> how systems perceive and utilize energy, not the underlying physics

## From single creatures to collective systems

As systems scale:
- individuals coordinate
- roles specialize
- actions distribute over time and space

This creates a new challenge:
> coordinating energy usage across many live creatures with partial information

At this point, direct perception is no longer enough. Systems require:
- shared abstractions
- transferable metrics
= coordination mechanisms

Energy remains the foundation — but new layers emerge on top of it.

## Engineering civilization: explicit control

With engineering, humans make this explicit:
- measure processes
- formalize models
- control transformations

We build:
- engines
- power systems
- computation infrastructure

Now energy becomes:
- precisely calculated
- deliberately directed
- massively scaled

But the core remains unchanged:
> all systems still operate through OCA loops under uncertainty

## The limitation that never disappears

Even in advanced systems:
- observations are incomplete
- models are approximations
- environments shift

So:
> we never operate on energy itself, only on our estimates of it

And those estimates:
- can be wrong
- can be outdated
- can be inconsistent across live creatures

## What energy really is

From an evolutionary perspective, we can finally compress the idea:

> The physical world consists of space, structures, and processes. Energy is an invariant abstract metric that live creatures use to estimate and predict the outcomes of interactions within that world.

And for live creature:
> Energy functions as a context-dependent measure of actionable capacity within an OCA loop.

## Final thought

From primitive organisms to engineering civilization, nothing fundamental has changed:
- systems observe
- systems compute
- systems act

Energy flows through all of it. But what determines survival and success is not energy alone.
> It is the ability to perceive, estimate, and direct it effectively under real-world constraints.

Everything else — intelligence, technology, economy — is built on top of that capability.
